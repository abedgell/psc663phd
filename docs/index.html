Course Description

Since the end of WWII, donor countries have spent over $7 trillion on foreign aid. Many of these projects seek to enhance human well-being through improved infrastructure, access to food, education opportunities, healthcare provision, broadened political freedoms, and increased security. Yet, the decisions behind when, where, and how to allocate foreign aid are embedded in domestic and international power dynamics. Donor countries make strategic choices about which countries to assist and which target areas to prioritize. Likewise, recipient governments have their own agendas and engage in strategies to capture foreign aid. This course examines these dynamics, with a particular emphasis on how the United States government makes foreign aid decisions. By the end of the semester, you will have foundational working knowledge that you can apply throughout your career in public service, academia, or the private sector.

Student Learning Objectives

By the end of the semester, you should be able to…

  1. Understand the evolution and goals of U.S. foreign aid policy
  2. Explain the political economy dynamics of development assistance
  3. Develop a research paper addressing an empirical puzzle related to U.S. foreign aid decisions or administration

Course Requirements

1. Participation (20%)

You are expected to attend and participate in all scheduled class meetings. You should arrive on time and be prepared to discuss the assigned material. This means that you need to actively engage with the required readings before class, asking questions such as:

  • What type of material is this (i.e. scholarly research, policy paper, editorial)?
  • What is the question or problem motivating this work?
  • What methods do the author(s) use?
  • What are the conclusions or main arguments?
  • How does this work relate to other materials we’ve discussed in class?
  • What are the core contributions and limitations of this work?

I strongly encourage you to prepare notes on the readings and bring them to class.

For each class session, you can earn up to five points toward your participation grade. To earn points, you must actively participate in the discussion. You will not earn participation credit for merely attending class. You may also lose participation credit if you arrive to class late or if you are not actively listening to the discussion (e.g., working on other assignments, doom scrolling) since this is disrespectful and potentially distracting. It is possible to earn a negative participation credit score for the day.

2. Leading Class Discussion (30%)

You will lead the class discussion on 13 September and another week of your choice. This assignment is intended to give you additional teaching experience, particularly at the graduate level. You should use about one hour to present the assigned readings using PowerPoint. Afterward, you will lead the other students in an activity designed to help the class better understand the material through active learning. For 13 September, Dr. Edgell will provide materials and instructions for the in-class activity. For the other week, you are responsible for developing the activity, in consultation with Dr. Edgell.

3. Research Paper (50%)

You will develop an original research project that investigates an empirical puzzle related to U.S. foreign aid decisions or administration. The project should thoroughly address the research question using qualitative, quantitative, or mixed methods. Your final paper and presentation should have a clearly articulated research question, review of relevant literature, preliminary theory, methodology, discussion of results, and conclusion. This is an opportunity to explore broader questions related to your possible dissertation research, for a conference paper, or article manuscript. To help you succeed at this assignment, we will have several meetings and interim deadlines to track your progress. These are summarized below.

Plagiarism and AI Technology

Learning to communicate your ideas effectively is an essential skill for any career path you may eventually take after graduation. While AI technology is exciting and offers opportunities to improve the efficiency of our research and writing, misusing this technology has negative consequences. Bots like ChatGPT are only accurate about 60-70% of the time and have been known to fabricate information. Using these tools to write your work for you is not only unethical, it renders you invisible. Your voice matters! Don’t let an AI silence you.

In line with UA’s Academic Misconduct policy, I expect you to generate your own work in this course. By submitting an assignment, you claim this work is entirely your own. Any work that you have paraphrased should be correctly cited. If you use more than three words in a row from someone else’s work, use quotation marks and cite the source, including the page number.

If you submit work generated by someone else or created by a computer application such as ChatGPT, this will be considered a violation of the Academic Misconduct policy and may be reported to the Department Chair or Dean’s office for review.

You may use word processing tools to check your spelling and grammar and assist with simple re-phrasing tasks. For example, I use the Grammarly application to help with my writing. It checks spelling and punctuation and sometimes provides advice on rephrasing sentences for clarity. This is a perfectly acceptable use of technology. However, having Grammarly’s AI assistant or similar tools write your work for you is unacceptable.
